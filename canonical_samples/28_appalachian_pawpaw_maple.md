# Appalachian Pawpaw & Maple

*America's forgotten fruit meets mountain sweetness*

**Difficulty:** OVERKILL — not because the technique is hard, but because pawpaws are nearly impossible to source. They show up for about two weeks in September, don’t ship, and spoil in ~48 hours. You either forage, know someone, or wait another year. The custard is standard. Getting the fruit is not.

**Total Time:** 6–8 hours (includes 4+ hours chilling, plus the year you waited for pawpaw season)

**Allergen Information:** Tree nuts (walnuts)

This one is for patient people. Pawpaws taste like banana, mango, and custard all at once—creamy, tropical, slightly strange in a good way. You can’t really buy them. You find them or you don’t.

Maple and black walnuts keep it rooted in Appalachia. It’s a regional combination built around an ingredient most people never get to use. That’s the whole point.

---

## Ingredients

**Pawpaw Custard Base:**
- 2 cups heavy cream
- 1 cup whole milk
- 2/3 cup granulated sugar
- 1/4 cup Grade A Dark or Very Dark maple syrup
- 4 egg yolks (NOT 6—we need the pawpaw to shine, not get buried under yolk richness)
- 1 cup pawpaw pulp (from about 4-6 pawpaws, seeds removed)
- 1 tsp vanilla extract
- 1 tbsp lemon juice (just to brighten it, not make it citrusy)
- Pinch of salt

**Black Walnut Maple Brittle:**
- 1 cup black walnut halves (or regular walnuts if you can't find black)
- 1/2 cup Grade A Dark maple syrup
- 2 tbsp butter
- 1/4 tsp salt
- 1/4 tsp vanilla extract

**Bourbon-Maple Glaze (optional but highly recommended):**
- 1/2 cup Grade A Very Dark or Grade B maple syrup
- 1/4 cup good bourbon (something you'd actually drink, not the bottom shelf stuff)
- 2 tbsp butter
- Pinch of salt

---

## Instructions

### Make the Black Walnut Maple Brittle (Do this first, it needs to cool)

1. **Toast the walnuts.** Spread the black walnuts on a baking sheet and toast at 350°F for 8–10 minutes until fragrant and slightly darkened. Don’t overdo it—they’re already strong. Let cool, then chop roughly into chunks.

2. **Make the brittle.** In a medium saucepan, combine maple syrup, butter, and salt. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, then reduce to medium. Cook, stirring occasionally, until it reaches 240°F (soft-ball stage), about 8–12 minutes. It will thicken and the bubbles will slow.

3. **Add walnuts and spread.** Remove from heat, stir in vanilla and chopped walnuts, and immediately spread onto a parchment-lined baking sheet. Work quickly—it sets fast. Let cool completely (at least 1 hour), then break into chunks. Store airtight.

---

### Make the Bourbon-Maple Glaze (if using)

4. **Reduce maple and bourbon.** Combine maple syrup and bourbon in a small saucepan. Simmer for 8–10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until reduced by about half and thickened. It should coat the back of a spoon.

5. **Finish with butter.** Remove from heat and whisk in butter and salt until smooth. Cool to room temperature, then refrigerate. It should be cold and thick before using.

---

### Make the Pawpaw Custard Base

6. **Prepare the pawpaws.** Cut in half, scoop out pulp, and remove all seeds. Mash smooth or blend briefly. Stir in lemon juice to prevent browning and brighten flavor. Set aside.

7. **Heat dairy and maple.** In a medium saucepan, combine cream, milk, half the sugar (about 1/3 cup), and maple syrup. Heat over medium until steaming with small bubbles at the edges. Do not boil.

8. **Whisk yolks.** In a bowl, whisk egg yolks, remaining sugar, and salt until pale and thick.

9. **Temper.** Slowly drizzle about 1 cup of the hot cream into the yolks while whisking constantly. Then pour the yolk mixture back into the saucepan.

10. **Cook to nappe.** Cook over medium-low heat, stirring constantly, until thick enough to coat the back of a spoon (170–175°F), about 5–8 minutes. Do not boil.

11. **Add pawpaw and vanilla.** Remove from heat and stir in pawpaw pulp and vanilla extract.

12. **Strain and chill.** Strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean bowl. Press solids to extract all liquid. Cover with plastic wrap directly on the surface and refrigerate until fully cold, at least 4 hours or overnight.

---

### Churn and Assemble

13. **Churn.** Churn the cold custard until soft-serve consistency. It will be pale yellow-green and slightly dense.

14. **Layer.** In a freezer-safe container, layer ice cream, bourbon-maple glaze (if using), and brittle chunks. Repeat to distribute evenly.

15. **Freeze.** Press plastic wrap or parchment onto the surface, cover, and freeze for at least 4 hours until firm.

## Notes

**About Pawpaws:**
Native to the Appalachians and parts of the Midwest. They ripen for a short window in September and don’t keep. You’ll need to forage, grow, or source locally. The pulp is creamy and tropical—banana, mango, custard, with hints of pineapple and melon.

**Why Only 4 Egg Yolks:**
Too many yolks mute the pawpaw. Four keeps the custard structured but lets the fruit come through clearly.

**Black Walnuts vs. Regular Walnuts:**
Black walnuts are stronger and more earthy. Use them if you can find them. Regular walnuts are milder but still work.

**Maple Syrup Grades:**
Use darker syrups (Grade A Dark or Very Dark). You want strong maple flavor.

**Make-Ahead:**
Brittle can be made days ahead. Glaze keeps for about a week refrigerated. Pawpaw base must be made the same day you get the fruit.

**About the Bourbon-Maple Glaze:**
Optional, but it adds depth. Most alcohol cooks off, leaving a richer maple flavor. Without it, reduce maple with butter for a simpler version.

**Why OVERKILL Difficulty:**
Not technique—timing and sourcing. Pawpaws are the constraint.

---

## What it tastes like

Creamy, tropical, and slightly funky in a way nothing else is. Banana and mango notes with a custard-like body. Maple adds warmth. The glaze brings depth. Walnut brittle adds crunch. Distinct, balanced, and hard to replicate without the fruit.
