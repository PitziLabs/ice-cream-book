# Miso Matcha

*Umami in ice cream (trust us)*

**Difficulty:** LEGIT — you’re sifting matcha, whisking it in a W or M (not circles, unless you enjoy clumps), dissolving miso properly, and timing additions into hot custard. None of this is complicated, but it does require attention. Skip steps and you’ll get grit. Rush it and you’ll regret it. Follow directions and you’re good.

**Total Time:** 6–8 hours across 2 days (toasting rice, overnight chill, plus 30–60 seconds of aggressive whisking that will absolutely tire your arm)

Yes, there’s miso in this. Yes, that sounds wrong. It isn’t.

White miso adds depth—it pushes the matcha forward, makes it taste more like itself, and adds a quiet umami layer. It’s not a sweet/savory gimmick. It’s subtle, and it works.

The toasted rice is a nod to genmaicha—green tea with toasted rice. That pairing has been around forever. This just freezes it.

You’ll probably doubt this when you read it. That’s expected. People always do. Then they taste it, go quiet for a second, and come back for more.

## Ingredients

**Base:**
- 2 cups heavy cream (small amount for miso, rest for custard)
- 1 cup whole milk (1/4 cup for matcha, rest for custard)
- 1/2 cup sugar
- 4 egg yolks
- 2.5 tbsp high-quality culinary or ceremonial matcha powder
- 2.5 tbsp white miso paste (shiro miso)
- Pinch of salt

**Toasted Rice Crispies:**
- 1 cup crispy rice cereal (like Rice Krispies)
- 1 tbsp butter
- 1 tbsp sugar
- Tiny pinch of salt

## Instructions

**Toasted Rice Crispies (make this first—it needs to cool):**

Melt butter in a large skillet over medium heat. Add the rice cereal and toast, stirring constantly for 3–4 minutes until golden and deeply nutty. You’ll see slight browning and smell the shift from bland to something closer to popcorn.

This step matters. You’re removing moisture, triggering Maillard reactions, and turning filler into flavor—same idea as genmaicha.

Sprinkle sugar and salt over the cereal. Stir for 1 more minute as the sugar melts and lightly coats everything. Spread on parchment to cool completely. It’ll crisp further as it cools. Store airtight until needed—these stay crunchy for days if kept dry.

**Prepare Matcha (and understand why this step matters):**

Matcha is extremely fine powder—around 5–10 microns—and coated in natural oils that repel water. If you dump it straight into liquid, it clumps. Badly. You’ll get gritty ice cream.

So: sift, then whisk properly.

Sift the matcha through a fine-mesh sieve into a bowl. Break up clumps gently with a spoon if needed. You want a smooth powder.

Heat 1/4 cup milk to 165–175°F. Hot, not boiling. Pour over the matcha.

Now whisk hard in a W or M pattern—not circular. Circular whisking creates a vortex that traps clumps. The zigzag motion forces better mixing and breaks particles apart.

Whisk aggressively for 30–60 seconds until completely smooth and bright green. No streaks, no specks. If there are clumps, keep going or target them directly.

You’re aiming for a thick, smooth, vividly green paste. Set aside.

**Prepare Miso (why this can’t be skipped):**

Miso is thick and stubborn. It won’t dissolve cleanly in custard on its own.

Warm 1/2 cup cream to about 100°F—just warm. Add the miso and whisk until fully smooth. No lumps, no streaks.

You should end up with a pale, uniform liquid. If you don’t, keep whisking. This step takes about a minute and saves you from uneven flavor later.

Set aside.

**Make Custard:**

Combine remaining milk, cream, and sugar in a saucepan. Heat until steaming. Make a standard custard with the egg yolks—temper, return to heat, cook to 170–175°F while stirring constantly.

As soon as you pull it off the heat, whisk in the matcha paste immediately. Timing matters. The heat helps it incorporate smoothly and evenly.

Whisk hard for 15–20 seconds until the custard is uniformly pale green.

Then whisk in the miso-cream mixture until fully combined. The color will lighten slightly.

Add a pinch of salt. This sharpens the umami and brings everything into focus.

Strain through a fine-mesh sieve to catch any stray clumps or egg bits. Cool over an ice bath, stirring occasionally.

Taste when cold. You want clear green tea flavor, not harsh bitterness. The miso should read as depth, not saltiness.

If it’s too bitter, you can add another tablespoon of sugar. If it feels flat, note it for next time.

Refrigerate at least 4 hours, preferably overnight. It improves with time.

**Churn:**

Churn to soft-serve consistency. It may take slightly longer than usual—matcha can slow freezing a bit. Stay patient.

In the final minute, add the cooled toasted rice crispies. Mix them in without crushing them.

Transfer to a container and freeze at least 4 hours until firm.

## Notes

**Matcha quality and why it matters:**

Quality shows. Ceremonial grade is smoother and sweeter. Culinary grade works well here. Avoid dull, brownish matcha—it tastes like regret.

Good matcha is vibrant green and smells fresh. Store it airtight in the freezer once opened. It oxidizes fast.

**Sifting and whisking are not optional:**

Matcha clumps because of its oil coating. Sifting separates particles. Heat helps hydration. Proper whisking distributes it.

Skip any part and you’ll get grit. Simple as that.

**White miso and why it works:**

Use white miso only. It’s milder, slightly sweet, and not aggressively salty.

It must be dissolved in warm cream first. Otherwise you’ll get uneven blobs.

Find it in the refrigerated section of Asian markets or good grocery stores. It keeps for months once opened.

**The umami synergy between matcha and miso:**

Both contain glutamates—umami compounds. Together, they amplify each other.

The result isn’t “savory ice cream.” It’s deeper, more complex matcha. The miso enhances without announcing itself.

That’s why it works.

**Toasted rice and the genmaicha connection:**

Genmaicha pairs green tea with toasted rice. This follows the same logic.

The rice adds warmth, nuttiness, and crunch. It’s not random—it’s grounded in a long-standing flavor pairing.

**Cultural context:**

Matcha comes from centuries of Japanese tea practice focused on precision and intention. That mindset carries through here—temperature, whisking, timing.

Miso has over a thousand years of history in Japanese cooking, built around fermentation and umami.

This recipe isn’t novelty. It’s applying those ideas in a different format.

**Make-ahead strategy:**

- Toasted rice crispies: up to 5 days ahead, airtight
- Custard base: day before churning (better flavor)
- Churn: day of serving or 4+ hours before

Break it up if you want: Day 1 crispies, Day 2 custard, Day 3 churn.

**Visual description:**

Soft, natural green—like a matcha latte. Not neon.

Golden toasted rice pieces throughout. Smooth base with crunchy contrast.

Looks clean, deliberate, and slightly unusual—in a good way.

**Allergen Information:** Contains soy (white miso paste).

**What it tastes like:**

Green tea, front and center—earthy, vegetal, clear. Then a deeper layer you can’t quite name. That’s the miso.

It’s not salty. Not obviously savory. Just more complex.

Crunchy toasted rice cuts through with warm, nutty notes. The whole thing is balanced, layered, and a little addictive.

You won’t fully pin it down. You’ll keep eating anyway.
